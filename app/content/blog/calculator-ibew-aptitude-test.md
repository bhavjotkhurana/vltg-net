# Can You Use a Calculator on the IBEW Aptitude Test?

**No. Calculators are not allowed on the IBEW aptitude test. You'll be given pencils and scrap paper to work the math by hand, and that's it.**

It sounds simple, but it catches a lot of people off guard, so let's walk through what it means for your test day and, more importantly, for how you get ready.

## The rule is strict

It's not a "leave it in your bag" situation; it's a "don't bring one at all" situation. Local JATCs are blunt about it. (Not sure what a JATC is? Here's a [quick guide to who's who](/blog/ibew-neca-njatc-jatc-explained).) The Warren Electrical JATC says "calculators may NOT be used during the test. Do not bring any type of calculator." The West Texas JATC goes a step further: "if you bring a watch with a built-in calculator, you will be asked to remove it during the test session."

So a smartwatch, a calculator watch, your phone: none of it comes out during the test. What you get is what every applicant gets: pencils and scrap paper, provided at the test center.

## Why they do it

The math section is 33 algebra-and-functions questions in 46 minutes. The point isn't whether you can punch numbers into a calculator; it's whether you actually understand how to work through an equation. Doing it by hand is the test.

The good news: the math tops out at about a high-school Algebra 1 level. There's no calculus, no trigonometry, no statistics. Everything on it is designed to be solvable by hand with clean-ish numbers. You are not expected to do brutal long division under pressure; you're expected to know the methods.

## What this means for how you prepare

If you've leaned on a calculator for years (and most people have), the fix isn't complicated, but you do have to practice the right way:

- **Do practice problems by hand, start to finish.** No phone, no calculator. Rebuild the muscle for arithmetic, fractions, and multi-step algebra on paper.
- **Get comfortable estimating.** A quick sanity-check ("this should be around 40") catches a lot of careless mistakes when you can't verify with a calculator.
- **Practice at the real pace.** That's about 84 seconds per question. Doing the math by hand *and* on the clock is the actual skill being tested.
