# What Math Is on the IBEW Aptitude Test?

**Short answer: the math section is 33 questions in 46 minutes, all at a high-school Algebra 1 level or below, and all by hand with no calculator. Expect arithmetic, fractions, decimals, linear equations, ratios, factoring, quadratics, and number series. There's no geometry, trigonometry, statistics, or calculus.**

If it's been a few years since a math class, that list might look intimidating. It shouldn't. The scope here is narrow and fixed, which means you can actually cover all of it. Let's go through exactly what shows up.

## The format

The math half of the IBEW aptitude test (given by the [electrical training ALLIANCE through your local JATC](/blog/ibew-neca-njatc-jatc-explained)) is 33 multiple-choice questions in 46 minutes. That's about 84 seconds a question, and you work it all out by hand: pencil and scrap paper, [no calculator](/blog/calculator-ibew-aptitude-test).

## The full topic list

Drawing from what local training committees tell applicants to study, here's what to be fluent in. It breaks into a few groups.

**Arithmetic foundations**

- Whole numbers and order of operations
- Positive and negative numbers (integers)
- Multiplying and dividing decimals
- Rational and irrational numbers

**Fractions**

- Adding, subtracting, and multiplying fractions
- Finding common denominators
- Working fractions and whole numbers together

**Core algebra**

- The distributive property
- Evaluating and simplifying expressions
- Function notation
- Exponents

**Equations**

- Solving linear equations
- Solving by substitution
- Inequalities

**Ratios and proportions**

- Equivalent ratios
- Setting up and solving proportions

**Factoring and quadratics**

- Factoring, including FOIL and multiplying factors
- Factoring quadratics
- The quadratic formula

**Patterns and graphs**

- Number series (finding the rule and the missing number)
- Plotting points and reading simple graphs

## What is not on the test

This is the reassuring part. The IBEW aptitude test does not cover geometry, trigonometry, statistics, or calculus. It stays inside basic arithmetic and Algebra 1. So if you've been dreading having to relearn a whole math degree, you can let that go. The real job is getting quick and confident with the list above, by hand.
